Safety for generalist robots is broader than constraint enforcement.
We argue it must include initiation authorization: deciding whether
the robot may commit its first irreversible social act under
uncertainty, by speech, reach, or approach. On many platforms the
harmful act is that first commitment, not a collision. Mapping
context, intent, and preference to pre-commitment decisions shows
why passive thresholds and end-to-end VLAs are structurally misaligned
with generalist deployment
\citep{rt2-2023,zhang2025safevla,ravichandran2025roboguard}.

The \textsc{pas} prototype implements the framing with a runtime split
between reversible probes, an authorization gate, and downstream
generation. Our study measures whether that split changes margin
distributions and user-reported awkwardness. If initiation
authorization becomes a peer topic to physical safety, reference
architectures should treat first-commit decisions as logged, tunable
objects separable from dialogue or motion planning. Benchmarks should
include latent receptivity, not only fluent continuation after a
channel is open. Red-teaming should cover embodied cold-start, where
jailbreaks surface as harmful first moves rather than toxic
mid-dialogue replies \citep{robey2024jailbreaking,kojima2025fmr}.
Naming the gap changes what ``safe generalist robot'' must mean as
these systems move into public and domestic space.
