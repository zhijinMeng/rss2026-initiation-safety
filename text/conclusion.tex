Generalist-robot safety should ask one more question before motion or
dialogue safety kick in: should the robot take its first hard-to-undo
social action now? We call that \emph{initiation authorization}. In
practice, harm often comes from speaking or reaching too soon---for
example, greeting someone who is on a phone---rather than from a
collision. A robot can be collision-free and still feel unsafe at first
contact. Our \textsc{pas} prototype enforces this with reversible
probes, an authorization gate, and only then dialogue generation. A
planned evaluation ($n{=}90$) will test whether that split changes margin
distributions and reported awkwardness. As these robots enter public and
home settings, treating initiation as a safety layer changes what
``safe generalist robot'' must mean.
