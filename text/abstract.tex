Safety for generalist robots is usually framed around motion or
dialogue. We argue a third layer is missing: deciding whether the
robot may take its first irreversible social act at all---a spoken
greeting, an uninvited grasp, an approach into personal space. We
call this \emph{initiation authorization}, a subclass of
social-interaction safety that current frameworks under-specify
\citep{akalin2021,weiss2023perceived,tian2021socialerrors}. Today's
stacks often skip this decision, treating an engagement score or
a single VLA rollout as permission ($e_t > \theta \Rightarrow
\textsc{act/speak}$). But observation is not consent. We situate
initiation authorization within generalist-robot safety, contrast it
with post-plan VLA guardrails that assume the
robot is already permitted to act
\citep{zhang2025safevla,kojima2025fmr,ravichandran2025roboguard,robey2024jailbreaking},
sketch \textsc{pas} (probe--authorize--speak) on a real robot,
and pose questions for the community.
