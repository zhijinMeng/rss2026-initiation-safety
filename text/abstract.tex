Safety for generalist robots is usually discussed in terms of motion or
dialogue. We argue a third question is missing: should the robot take
its first hard-to-undo social action at all, such as a greeting, an uninvited
grasp, or stepping into someone's space? We call this \emph{initiation
authorization}. Current frameworks rarely treat it as a separate safety
layer. Today's stacks often skip this step: a high engagement score or
a confident VLA rollout is treated as permission to act. But seeing a
person is not the same as having their consent to be addressed. We
place initiation authorization within generalist-robot safety, contrast
it with post-plan VLA guardrails, and sketch \textsc{pas} (probe--authorize--speak) on a
real robot with a three-policy evaluation protocol.
